Fashion in Russia in the 1870s: The Real Look

The novel Anna Karenina was serialized between 1875 and 1877. During this period, women’s fashion was transitioning from the wide skirts of the mid-19th century to more form-fitting silhouettes with elaborate detailing.

Key Fashion Features (1873–1877)

  • Bustle (or tournure): A padded undergarment worn to create a protruding shape at the back of the skirt.
  • Trains: Skirts often extended into long, trailing trains.
  • Rich Ornamentation: Dresses were highly decorative, featuring drapery, bows, lace, embroidery, and layers of fabric.
  • Layered Looks: Corsets, chemises, petticoats, and bustles were worn beneath outer garments.

Authentic garments from the time show how intricate and structured these dresses were—true masterpieces of tailoring.

Film Costumes vs. Historical Accuracy

Let’s take a look at five notable film versions of Anna Karenina and how they represented Anna's style:

1. Love (1927) — Greta Garbo & John Gilbert

  • Fashion Context: Costumes reflected 1920s style rather than 1870s authenticity.
  • Fun Fact: The film had two different endings—one happy (for American audiences), one tragic (for Europe).
  • Costume Designer: Gilbert Clark.
  • Note: The chemistry between Garbo and Gilbert gave the film added appeal, even if the costumes were anachronistic.

2. Anna Karenina (1935) — Greta Garbo

  • Costume Designer: Adrian (Adrian Adolph Greenburg), known for his glamorous and stylized Hollywood creations.
  • Style: Loosely inspired by 1870s fashion but heavily dramatized with ruffles and lace.
  • Critique: Some felt that the costumes prioritized Hollywood drama over historical accuracy.

3. Anna Karenina (1948) — Vivien Leigh

  • Costume Designer: Cecil Beaton, also known for My Fair Lady.
  • Highlights: Despite post-war fabric rationing in Britain, Beaton’s designs appeared lush and luxurious.
  • Visuals: Beaton’s photographs of Leigh in costume were featured in British Vogue.

4. Anna Karenina (1997) — Sophie Marceau

  • Production: A co-production by Mel Gibson’s Icon Productions.
  • Costume Designer: Maurizio Millenotti, who trained at the legendary Tirelli tailoring workshops in Rome.
  • Accuracy: This version is praised for being the most historically faithful, with costumes and accessories true to the 1870s.
  • Color Palette: Anna’s costumes use a black-and-white theme—black dominating during her downfall, and white during happier times.

5. Anna Karenina (2012) — Keira Knightley

  • Costume Designer: Jacqueline Durran.
  • Controversy: Despite receiving an Oscar nomination, many critics argued the costumes were theatrical and did not match the period.
  • Fashion Fusion: Chanel accessories were used, giving the film a modern couture feel rather than strict period adherence.
